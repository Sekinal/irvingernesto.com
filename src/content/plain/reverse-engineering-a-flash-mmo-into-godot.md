---
title: 'Bringing a Beloved Space Game Back From the Dead'
summary: 'When web browsers switched off Flash, a whole era of online games vanished overnight. This is how I took apart a much-loved multiplayer space game, recovered its art and its rules, and rebuilt it from scratch so people could play it again.'
---

For about twenty years, a huge number of online games lived inside something called Flash. It was the technology that made most browser games work, the little space-shooters and puzzle games and worlds you could jump into without installing anything. And then, at the end of 2020, the companies that make web browsers agreed to switch Flash off for good. It had become a security problem, and keeping it alive was not worth the risk.

The trouble is that when they flipped that switch, thousands of games went dark all at once. Not just paused. Gone. Many of them were multiplayer worlds that lived on the internet, and most were never rebuilt anywhere else. One afternoon they worked, and the next they simply did not, and there was no going back.

One of those games was a space adventure I loved. You flew a little ship around a galaxy, upgraded your weapons, and battled other real people. When it disappeared, I decided I did not want to just miss it. I wanted to bring it back.

## Restoring a demolished building from its photographs

The best way to picture what I did is to imagine a beautiful old building that has been torn down. You cannot un-demolish it. But suppose you still had every photograph of it, the original architect's blueprints, and even the paint samples from every room. With enough patience, you could rebuild the whole thing, faithfully, brick for brick.

That is exactly the situation I was in. The old game was gone, but the pieces it had been made from were still recoverable if you knew how to look. So the first job was careful detective work: taking the original game apart to pull out everything it was built from.

Out came the artwork, every drawing of every ship, weapon, and explosion, thousands of little pictures in all. Out came the sounds, the engine hums and laser blasts and music. And most surprising of all, out came the rulebook: the exact numbers that governed how a ship sped up, how it slowed, how much damage a weapon did, how enemies decided to chase or flee. I did not have to guess at any of it. I could read the original rules and copy them down exactly, the way you would trace a blueprint rather than sketch a building from memory.

## Rebuilding it in a modern workshop

With the recovered pieces in hand, I rebuilt the game from the ground up in a modern game engine, which is essentially a well-equipped workshop for making games. This new version does not depend on Flash at all, so it will keep working for as long as ordinary software does. Same ships, same art, same feel, on foundations that will not crumble.

I kept the details honest on purpose. The old game updated its world about thirty times a second, so the new one does too. A ship gets faster and drifts to a stop using the very same numbers the original used. When you rebuild something you love, the small stuff is not small. It is the difference between a copy and the real thing.

## The referee who keeps everyone honest

There was one more piece I refused to skip, and it is the piece that makes it a real multiplayer game rather than a single-player imitation.

In any online game where people compete, someone has to be trusted to keep score. If each player's own computer were allowed to decide where that player was, how much health they had, and who won a fight, then a dishonest player could simply lie. They could tell everyone else they had full health, or that they were somewhere they were not, and there would be no way to argue.

So the game has a referee. It lives on a central computer, not on any player's machine, and it is the only one allowed to say what is actually true. Your computer does not announce "I moved here." It just says "I pressed the accelerate key," and the referee works out where that really puts you and tells everyone. Nobody can cheat by lying about themselves, because their own word was never what counted.

There is a clever trick hidden in this, because waiting for a distant referee to answer every keypress would make the game feel sluggish. So your ship moves the instant you steer it, guessing where it should go, and then quietly corrects itself the moment the referee's official answer arrives. Most of the time the guess is right and you never notice. When it is a little off, the ship glides smoothly to the correct spot instead of jerking. You get a game that feels instant and stays honest at the same time.

## Why it was worth it

A game is more than a fond memory. It is a specific set of pictures, sounds, and rules, and if you save those, you save the game. Flash took this one away, but it left behind everything needed to rebuild it, for anyone patient enough to gather the pieces back up. I was patient enough. Slowly, the lights are coming back on.
